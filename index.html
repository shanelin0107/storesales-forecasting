Dataset

Data Overview

The dataset covers Corporación Favorita, Ecuador’s largest grocery chain. Training data spans Feb 2013 to Aug 15 2017 (~3 million rows); the forecast target is Aug 16–31 2017 (28,513 rows across 1,782 store–family pairs).

Scale

54 Stores × 33 Families

1,782 concurrent time series. Stores span 22 cities across Ecuador. Families range from PRODUCE and BEVERAGES to BOOKS and BABY CARE.

Auxiliary Data

Rich External Context

Oil prices (Ecuador is oil-dependent), national/regional/local holidays, daily transaction counts, promotion calendars, and store metadata.

Challenge

Structural Break

The April 2016 earthquake caused a ~3-week disruption to purchasing behavior — handled via an explicit dummy variable to prevent contaminating lag features.

Fig 2 — Total daily sales across all stores (Feb 2013 – Aug 2017). The April 2016 earthquake dip is visible mid-series. The overall trend is gradual growth with strong weekly and annual seasonality.
Approach

Methodology

1 — Feature Engineering

40+ features across six categories, computed once and stored as a Parquet feature store (outputs/train_fe.parquet), reused across all model iterations.

Temporal

Lag & Rolling Features

Sales lags (lag_1–63, lag_364/371/728), 7/14/28-day rolling mean and standard deviation.

Calendar

Date & Holiday Features

Day of week, month, week of year, is_weekend, is_month_start (paycheck effect +12–18%), days_to/after_holiday matched by store geography.

External

Oil & Promotions

Daily oil price + 7/28-day moving average. Forward-looking onpromotion flag (available for test set) plus lagged promotion history.

2 — Per-Family Architecture

Replaced a single global model with 33 independent LightGBM models — one per product family. Each model is trained, validated, and predicts entirely within its own product category.

Fig 3 — Sales distributions by product family. PRODUCE, BEVERAGES, and MEATS have fundamentally different scales, variance, and skew. A global model cannot serve all simultaneously; per-family models each learn their own patterns.

Dense consecutive lags (lag_16~63): Providing all 48 consecutive lag days lets each tree model implicitly learn any moving-average pattern — trends, momentum, mean reversion — without manual feature engineering.

3 — Recursive Forecasting

Direct forecasting requires a minimum lag of 16 days, losing lag_7. Recursive forecasting predicts one day at a time, using each prediction as a lag for the next step:

Aug 16 → predict → stored in buffer
Aug 17 → predict using lag_1 = Aug 16 prediction
Aug 18 → predict using lag_1 = Aug 17, lag_2 = Aug 16
     ⋮
Aug 31 → predict using lag_1 … lag_15 all from buffer
Fig 4 — Autocorrelation function. Sharp spikes at lag 7, 14, 21, 28 confirm strong weekly consumer behavior cycles. Making lag_7 available via recursive forecasting was the single largest improvement (0.41781 → 0.397).

4 — Zero-Sales Post-Processing

If a store–family combination had zero sales for the past 3–7 consecutive days, force predictions to 0. No retraining required — applied as a final filter on the submission file.

Fig 5 — Zero-sales rate by store and product family. BOOKS, BABY CARE, and MAGAZINES have high zero-sale rates. Models always output small positive numbers; zeroing them out corrects a systematic positive bias.
Insights

Key Findings

Fig 6 — Average sales by day of week. Weekends and Fridays consistently peak; Tuesdays are lowest. This weekly rhythm is the dominant short-term demand signal.
  • 1

    Weekly cycles dominate short-term demand

    Sales peak on weekends and Fridays, trough on Tuesdays. lag_7 was the top-ranked feature in every per-family model.

    In production: ensure your data pipeline delivers T-1 day sales with <24 h latency so short-lag features are always available for daily retraining.
  • 2

    Product category matters more than store location

    Splitting by product family gave larger gains than any store-level segmentation. Demand patterns are shaped more by what is sold than where.

    Build category-level forecasting pipelines rather than one-size-fits-all models.
  • 3

    Zero-sales detection is an inventory signal

    127 store–family combinations had 7+ consecutive zero-sale days — representing discontinued SKUs, stockouts, or seasonal gaps.

    Auto-flag these for store manager review: stockout → trigger replenishment; discontinued → cancel pending purchase orders.
  • 4

    Year-ago signals are useful but fragile

    lag_364/371 captures annual seasonality. Restricting training to 2015+ data (fresher) hurt performance — reducing sample size outweighed the freshness benefit.

    Use full historical data with year-ago lag features rather than truncating the training window.
  • 5

    Macroeconomic context belongs in retail models

    Oil price was a consistently non-trivial feature. Ecuador’s government revenue is oil-dependent, affecting public-employee wages — a large consumer segment — with a 1–3 month lag.

    For international deployment: identify the local macro indicator most correlated with consumer spending (fuel price, unemployment rate, consumer confidence index).
  • 6

    Holiday effects require geographic precision

    National holidays lift all stores uniformly. Regional and local holidays affect only stores in the matching state/city. Mismatching features introduces noise.

    Maintain a store–geography mapping and apply holiday features at the correct granularity.
  • 7

    Structural breaks need explicit handling

    The April 2016 earthquake caused a ~3-week disruption to purchasing patterns. Without a dummy variable, the model learns incorrect lag patterns from that period.

    Maintain a “disruption calendar” for known structural breaks (natural disasters, strikes, pandemic periods) and add indicator variables.
Production

Real-World Deployment Recommendations

Insights from competition modeling that translate directly to production grocery forecasting systems:

Data Latency Daily sales data with <24 h lag required to use lag_1~7 features. Late data = degraded short-term accuracy.
Retraining Cadence Weekly retraining recommended; monthly is the minimum given strong weekly cycles.
Inventory Auto-flag store–SKU pairs with 7+ consecutive zero-sales days for manager review: stockout vs. discontinued.
Promotions Forward-looking onpromotion calendar (known in advance) is high-value. Maintain it rigorously as a first-class input.
Model Granularity Per-category models. Consider per-store-type models if fewer than ~10 stores per type.
Forecast Horizon Confidence degrades after day 7 in recursive mode. Widen safety stock buffers for day 14+ forecasts.
Macro Features Include the local macro indicator most correlated with consumer purchasing power (oil, wages, consumer confidence).
